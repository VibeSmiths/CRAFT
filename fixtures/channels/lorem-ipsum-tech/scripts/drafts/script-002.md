**Alex:** Picture this: you're standing at the edge of a black hole. Behind you, the entire observable universe is compressed into a tiny bright ring. In front of you... nothing. Absolute nothing.

Except — and this is the part that broke Stephen Hawking's brain — it's not actually nothing.

### The Great Escape

Here's the standard explanation: black holes are so massive that nothing can escape their gravity. Not even light. Hence: black.

But in 1974, Hawking realized something bizarre. Quantum mechanics says that empty space isn't really empty. It's constantly fizzing with pairs of particles that pop into existence and immediately annihilate each other.

Now imagine this happens right at the edge of a black hole — the event horizon. One particle falls in. The other escapes. From the outside, it looks like the black hole just *emitted* a particle.

*(Animation showing particle pair at event horizon)*

**Alex:** This is Hawking radiation. And it means black holes aren't perfectly black. They glow. Very, very faintly.

### The Slow Death

Here's where it gets wild. Every time a particle escapes, the black hole loses a tiny bit of mass. Over incomprehensible timescales — we're talking longer than the current age of the universe — a black hole will eventually evaporate completely.

The biggest black holes will take 10^100 years to disappear. That number is so large it makes the age of the universe look like a rounding error.

**Alex:** So black holes aren't eternal cosmic prisons. They're temporary. Just... very, very patient.
